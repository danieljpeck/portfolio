Overview

Zenplace is a property management company that needed to keep user trust and attraction on landing pages. I designed a way to articulate the brand value, as well as a style guide and brand guideline for a cohesive expansion of pages.

Exploring the market

What is working and what is not

    tl;dr

  • People are frustrated with the whole process of property management, not just one aspect

  • Users are confused and suspicious of site

I began by conducting a survey and performing user interviews to get a climate check on what aspects of property management are the most important to property owners.

The survey pointed to people wanting responsive communication, fast maintenance fixes, and quick filling of properties with tenants. The aspects to articulate in design came rather easily in that regard.

Usability was also a factor that I wanted to incorporate into the designs so my partner and I began running some initial testing and heuristic evaluations. We followed the LEMErS for diagnosing and evaluating heuristics. Then we conducted some usability testing to better understand the pain points and information flow that users experience.



During our testing, our responses had strong through lines. Many users were unable to perform the tests that we thought most important as the navigation and usability confused them to a degree where they gave up. Others thought the website was a scam and was a front for something mysterious or dubious. And most importantly, no one could accurately or effectively convey what Zenplace did, how it would work, and why they should start using them.



Pivoting the design

How do you build trust through a website

    tl;dr

  • Users felt uneasy because of the lack of humans

  • People wanted to know what would happen before they signed off

After we conducted our evaluation, we thought it important to look at the problems Zenplace had holistically. Our new set of problems left us with a great deal of work to do. The true problems:

    - Significant usability and navigation obstacles
    - Poor communication of value proposition
    - Cryptic process and untrustworthiness

My partner and I developed a critical information flow to help guide our decisions and designs. It lays out the most necessary questions that users ask themselves, consciously or subconsciously before they are ready to sign off on a product. The discovery aspect is understanding the process and description of Zenplace, while the validation aspect is the subconscious and gut feelings that guided the real onboarding, related to a deep value proposition and trust.



Designing for usability

Best practices for the discovered pain points

    tl;dr

  • There are standards of content that users expect

  • Information should be structured with critical info in the header, and all else in the footer

The baseline that we had to design first was the information architecture. The site led users to different locations and the global navigation would change depending on which page the user was on. My partner and I designed a more intuitive navigation using the results of our usability tests and a card sorting exercise we performed with users. We included the elements directly related to the critical information flow on the header navigation while adding other supplemental materials to the footer, aligned with what users are already familiar with.



Giving the copy a voice

Zenful voices

    tl;dr

  • Copy should reflect the brand value AND call to action: Stress-free property management

  • The copy necessitates being well thought out and catchy

The copy of the website either was unclear or not available. While Rahul Mewawalla had a strong vision and value proposition for Zenplace, our usability tests uncovered how disconnected that vision was with how users were perceiving the site.

Laying out the content

Not what, but why

    tl;dr

  • Bright visuals and white space help give a more friendly, calm feeling

  • Callouts and annotation need to be very specific to enable development

After creating a copy guide and information architecture, my partner and I needed to articulate all the information we gathered into a cohesive layout. We were given some creative freedom in layouts and colors, however, we had to work with the brand colors and preserve the bootstrap grid layout of the site.

We plotted some of the interactions and modules that would have the important content from our research prominently displayed while maintaining the aspects and components that our stakeholders thought most important. After performing rounds of usability testing, we iterated our designs to be more familiar and intuitive . After performing rounds of usability testing, we iterated our designs to be more familiar, then received design approval.

Once these were approved we began to start making low and high fidelity mockups for our supervisor to approve and give to development. We packaged some of our high fidelity mockups, with annotated wireframes, and a design spec guide to give guidance to the development team.





Reflections

How to think for the future

    tl;dr

  • Planning for expansion is part of the startup cycle

  • Documenting design styles is helpful both for the designer and development

In order to help Zenplace with future expansion, my partner and I thought about what to give to our client. We wanted Zenplace to be able to expand and grow with new pages and features while still maintaining a cohesive tone and brand. Here, a style guidelines and brand guidelines seemed appropriate to create, as to allow the same tone of copy and visual thought to remain pervasive throughout the site.

Zenplace offers so much, and I attempted to create a ways and paths to translate that information as easily as possible.
